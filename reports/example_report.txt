 ABSTRACT  
This work consists of two main components. The first is a literature review on text generation through 
Large Language Models (LLMs) and hybrid deep -learning architectures, covering their evolution, 
applications across domains, representative model examples, and associated ethical considerations. 
This review provides a conceptual foundation for understanding modern generative systems but remains independent from the dataset, methods, and experiments used in the subsequent project.  
The second component develops a complete machine -learning pipeline for diabetes prediction using 
the CDC Health Indicators dataset, combining traditional models, deep -learning architectures, and 
ensemble methods. After thorough pre -processing —including SMO TE balancing, outlier capping, 
feature engineering, and scaling —a broad set of models was evaluated, ranging from CNN –RNN and 
Transformer- based hybrids to tree -boosting classifiers. While the tuned CNN –RNN model performed 
best among the neural networks (AU C ≈ 0.917), gradient -boosting ensembles such as XGBoost, 
LightGBM, and the final stacking model consistently achieved the highest accuracy, exceeding 0.90. 
Statistical tests (ANOVA, Chi-square, Cramér’s V) further validated key feature relationships. Overall, 
the results reaffirm that boosting- based ensembles remain the strongest choice for structured health 
data, with deep -learning hybrids serving as competitive baselines.

1.2. Evolution and Advancements in Text Generation  
Text- generation research has advanced beyond foundational architectures into deeper optimisation 
principles and multilingual challenges. The following developments represent the major 
advancements not covered earlier in Table 1 . 
• Scaling Laws  - Kaplan et al. (2020) showed that loss improves predictably with parameter 
count, dataset size, and compute. However, Hoffmann et al. (2022) (Chinchilla scaling) 
demonstrate that many LLMs are under -trained, wasting compute. These scaling laws explain 
GPT-3’s perplexity improvements relative to GPT- 2 and the efficiency of models like Chinchilla 
and PaLM.  
 
• Tokenisation & Vocabulary  - Multilingual LLM performance strongly depends on tokeni zer 
coverage. Huang et al. (2025) emphasise that XLM -V’s vocabulary re -engineering reduces 
token fragmentation and improves low -resource generative quality.  
 
• Empirical Anchoring  -  
o GPT-3 (175B) achieves ~20 –30 perplexity on WikiText -103.  
o T5-11B achieves BLEU 43+ on WMT- 14 EN→DE.  
o mT5 -XL achieves strong multilingual ROUGE -1 for translation and summarisation tasks.  
o Hybrid models yield consistent performance boosts of 2 –5% in vision tasks (Ambar, 
2025) and >10% improvements in biomedical time- series detection (Alghieth, 2025) . 
  
1.3. Examples and Influence of T ext Generation  Models  
Modern  text- generation examples include GPT -based  chatbots, automated clinical reporting systems, 
multilingual summarisation tools, email drafting assistants, and conversational AI agents . Hybrid CNN –
RNN –Transformer designs further influence generative modelling by enabling richer feature 
extraction.  For instance, Transformer– CNN hybrids designed for ECG anomaly detection (Alghieth, 
2025) demonstrate how CNN -driven local pattern extraction can complement self- attention in 
domains requiring precise structural modelling . Analogously, hybrid CNN –Transformer architectures 
in NLP can enhance stylistic coherence, local phrasing, and robustness to noisy inputs . This cross-
domain evidence reinforces the value of hybrid architectures for text generation, where models must 
similarly combine local linguistic cues with global discourse planning to produce coherent and context -
appropriate outputs.

1.5. Critical Evaluation   
Across the reviewed literature, several trends and limitations emerge.  
• Transformers vs RNNs - Transformers offer superior global reasoning but suffer from 
quadratic attention costs and long- context bottlenecks, while RNNs remain advantageous in 
memory -constrained streaming settings . 
 
• Transformers vs Hybrid Models  - Hybrid models outperform single -backbone networks 
(Ambar, 2025; Alghieth, 2025), but introduce computational complexity and parameter-
integration challenges.  
 
• Monolingual vs Multilingual PLMs  - GPT-style monolingual LLMs excel in high -resource 
languages but lack cross -lingual robustness. Multilingual LLMs (Huang et al., 2025) confront 
tokenisation bottlenecks and unfair performance disparities.  
 
• Scaling vs Architecture  - Scaling laws suggest predictable performance gains from increasing 
model size, but hybrid architectures show that architectural optimisation sometimes yields 
more efficient improvements than brute -force scale.

1.6. Conclusion  
Text generation has evolved from statistical template- based modelling to large -scale Transformer 
architectures capable of fluent, controlled generation across multiple domains. Seminal innovations —
including self -attention, scaling laws, optimised tokenisat ion, and hybrid CNN –RNN –Transformer 
design —underpin modern generative capabilities. Yet significant challenges remain, particularly 
regarding hallucination, privacy leakage, multilingual inequity, adversarial robustness, and 
environmental sustainability. H ybrid architectures, as evidenced by Ambar (2025) and Alghieth (2025), 
offer complementary advantages that can enhance efficiency, robustness, and interpretability. Future work must prioritise long -context modelling, equitable multilingual performance, transparent hybrid 
architectures, and compute -efficient scaling to ensure that text -generation systems remain reliable 
and socially responsible .

 2. Problem Specification (Task 3b)  
2.1. Introduction  
This study addresses diabetes prediction as a supervised binary classification task  using the CDC 
Diabetes dataset, which contains over 194825  samples and 2 7 behavioural, demographic, and clinical 
features. A comprehensive set of classification models was implemented, including Decision Tree, 
Logistic Regression, KNN, Random Forest, AdaBoost, XGBoost, LightGBM, MLP, RNN -LSTM, CNN -
LSTM, Hybrid Transformer and CNN, Hybrid CNN- RNN and Voting/Stacking ensembles.  
To improve predictive accuracy, the workflow incorporated feature engineering, SMOTE balancing, 
hyperparameter tuning, and ensemble learning. Overfitting was controlled through cross -validation, 
early stopping, dropout, regularisation, and depth constraint s. Model optimisation leveraged 
RandomizedSearchCV, scaling, outlier handling, and domain -informed interactions. The objective is to 
build a high -performance, generalisable system for diabetes risk classification . 
 
2.2. Real -World Problem Definition

(Naureen et al., 2023) show that CNNs and RNNs often underperform on tabular clinical data unless heavily regularised, lacking interpretability and robustness analyses.

Feature –target relationships were assessed using multiple statistical techniques. Pearson correlations 
were computed to rank continuous predictors by association strength with diabetes status. 
Categorical variables were evaluated using Chi- square tests, su pplemented by Cramér’s V to quantify

4. Model Development  
4.1. Stacking Ensemble (XGBoost + LightGBM + CatBoost + Random Forest ) 
The stacking ensemble combines XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost and Random Forest to exploit 
complementary gradient -boosting strategies for tabular health data. The approach is directly 
motivated by Liu et al. (2022), who demonstrated that stacking heterogeneous learners significantly 
improves diabetes -related prediction performance by reducing model bias and variance through 
meta -learning . Arman et al. (2025)  similarly highlights the superiority of ensemble -based architectures 
for diabetes prediction tasks comp ared with single models . CatBoost was incorporated based on 
recent findings that ordered boosting mitigates target leakage and handles categorical structure more 
effectively than traditional gradient boosting.  Across all reviewed studies, no prior work  combined 
XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost and Random Forest  within a unified stacking ensemble for diabetes 
prediction. The architecture developed in this project therefore represents a novel configuration 
relative to the existing literature and achieved the highe st performance  among all models evaluated .

The Transformer + CNN hybrid model was selected to combine the Transformer’s strength in 
modelling global, long -range feature dependencies with the CNN’s ability to extract local metabolic 
feature motifs from tabular health -indicator data. This design is d irectly motivated by Chen et al.

networks in blood -glucose prediction by capturing multi -parameter interactions more effectively than 
recurrent models. Their findings highlight that CNN layers provide robust feature extraction while the 
Transformer offers superior representation learning through multi- head self -attention, reducing 
information loss across heterogeneous physiological inputs. Although prior studies apply thi s hybrid 
approach mainly to glucose time -series for Type -1 diabetes, no existing work evaluates a CNN -
Transformer configuration on CDC -based tabular diabetes prediction. Implementing this hybrid 
architecture therefore extends the existing literature into a  new data domain and yields improved 
predictive stability compared with other neural baselines in this project .

performance on the CDC Health Indicators dataset. Individual boosting models  such as XGBoost

 5. Conclusion  
After completing all experiments across traditional machine -learning, deep -learning, and ensemble -
based models, several key insights emerged. First, hyperparameter tuning proved crucial for deep 
architectures: increasing convolutional depth, lowering the l earning rate, and strengthening 
regularisation improved the CNN –RNN model’s class -wise behaviour even though its overall accuracy 
remained stable at 0.8309. Second, the results reinforce a well- established pattern in clinical tabular 
prediction tasks —gradient -boosting ensembles such as XGBoost and LightGBM consistently 
outperform neural networks, with the stacking ensemble achieving the highest performance (Accuracy = 0.9069, AUC = 0.9639) due to its ability to model heterogeneous feature interactions and handle data imbalance effectively. Finally, although the optimized CNN –RNN hybrid emerged as the 
strongest deep -learning baseline, the persistent gap between neural and boosting models highlights 
a fundamental challenge: adapting architectures designed for spatial or sequential inputs to 
structured medical datasets where feature relationships lack natural order. These findings underscore the importance of model –data alignment, rigorous hyperparameter calibration, and suggest future 
work should explore tabula r-specific deep learning approaches, such as TabNet, NODE, or hybrid 
ensemble –DL (Deep Learning) systems, to further bridge this performance gap.

    chi2 = stats.chi2_contingency( confusion)[0 ]

    r, k = confusion. shape
